Behavioral sensing and cognitive aging

Coupled to Time, Stuck in Space: dissociable behavioral dimensions in MCI and dementia

Continuous smart-home sensing splits behavioral aging into two largely independent axes, and shows working memory tracks how varied your days are, not how much space you use.

with Diane J. Cook and Maureen Schmitter-Edgecombe, Washington State University. ← All research
In one minute
  1. QuestionIs behavioral organization at home a single reflection of cognitive capacity, or several dissociable dimensions the field has been blurring together?
  2. ApproachContinuously monitor 35 older adults at home, separate day-similarity (routine) from spatial complexity, and correlate each with N-back working memory.
  3. ResultCapacity tracks day-similarity (significant negative, about 18% of variance) but not spatial complexity; MCI keeps a near-rigid daily clock (0.94, just below dementia's 0.95) while contracting to the smallest daily footprint (7.7 zones).
  4. Why it mattersRising routine may be an early warning to watch for, not a comfort to preserve, which reframes what home monitoring is for.

The question and why it is hard

Cognitive aging is almost always measured as internal capacity: what a person can do when a clinician prompts them to remember words, copy a figure, or count backward. That framing treats the environment as background. But a competing view holds that cognition is relational, a fit between person and setting rather than a property of the brain alone. If that is right, then the way someone actually organizes their days at home carries information that a test in a clinic cannot.

The trouble is that "behavioral organization" is not one thing, and the field has largely treated it as one thing. Prior smart-home work mostly feeds movement features into classifiers to predict a diagnosis. That answers "can we tell who is impaired," but not "what dimension of lived behavior is actually changing." Two people can look identical on a coarse activity summary and inhabit completely different behavioral worlds.

The move

The whole study turns on pulling apart two things that get conflated: day-similarity (how much your Monday resembles your Tuesday, resembles your Wednesday) and spatial complexity (how broadly you use the physical space of your home). You can use few rooms in endlessly repetitive ways, or many rooms in endlessly repetitive ways. Routine and spatial richness are logically separable, so we refused to fold them into a single "activity" score and instead asked whether cognitive capacity relates to one and not the other.

Takeaway The gap is not detection; it is that "behavioral organization" hides at least two independent axes, and nobody had shown capacity relates to them differently.

The setup, and the reasoning behind the choices

Thirty-five community-dwelling older adults were monitored in their own homes through the CASAS Smart Home project: 15 healthy older adults (HOA), 9 with subjective cognitive decline (SCD), 7 with mild cognitive impairment (MCI), and 4 with dementia. Homes carried passive infrared motion sensors, door sensors, and other ambient sensors, no audio or video. Monitoring ran from 4 days to 22 months, median 8 months, for more than 2 million sensor events in total.

The decision

Everyone lived alone in a single-resident home. That is not incidental. With two residents, a motion sensor cannot say whose motion it is, and the location signal becomes a blend. Restricting to solo dwellers keeps every zone reading attributable to the target person. It costs sample size, but it buys a clean signal, and this study is about the shape of one person's days.

Raw sensor firings are discrete and jittery, so events were passed through an Unscented Kalman Filter to produce continuous position estimates, then discretized into 1-meter grid zones. Because homes ranged from 400 to 1800 square feet (15 to 72 distinct zones, median 38), spatial metrics were normalized to each home's available space.

The worry

A bigger home mechanically produces more zones, more entropy, more range. Without normalization, "spatial complexity" would partly just measure square footage, and any correlation with cognition would be confounded by real estate. Normalizing within each home's footprint is the guard against that, though as the limits section admits, it is not a complete one.

Cognitive capacity was assessed with the 1-back working-memory task, delivered by smartwatch prompts, with mean accuracy per participant ranging from 83% to 99%. From the position stream we computed four behavioral quantities: day-similarity (for each hour, the modal zone; then the mean pairwise agreement across all pairs of days, requiring at least 5 usable days); a composite behavioral complexity index (spatial entropy, unique cells visited, and physical extent, z-scored and averaged); temporal coupling (how consistently the person is in the hour's dominant zone across days); and spatial range (unique zones visited per day). Group differences used Kruskal-Wallis tests given the unequal, small groups; capacity relationships used Pearson correlations on the continuous individual-level data.

Why not this

The complexity index deliberately captures spatial complexity, not task difficulty. Washing counters back and forth across a kitchen scores high; doing taxes at one desk scores low, despite the cognitive load. We resisted the tempting shorthand that "more movement equals more cognitively engaged," because it would have quietly smuggled the answer into the measure.

Two dimensions the data pulls apart

The first result is a dissociation between the two behavioral axes in how they relate to working memory. Behavioral complexity showed no significant relationship with N-back accuracy. Day-similarity showed a significant negative one: people with higher working-memory accuracy tended to live more varied days, not more spatially expansive ones.

  • Cognitive capacity and day-similarity: significant negative correlation, accounting for roughly 18% of variance.
  • Cognitive capacity and spatial complexity: no significant relationship.
  • Day-similarity and complexity were only weakly correlated with each other, confirming they are largely independent axes rather than two views of one thing.
The test

The clean way to show two measures are distinct is to show they behave differently against a third variable while barely correlating with each other. That is exactly the pattern here: capacity latches onto day-similarity, ignores complexity, and the two behavioral axes are nearly orthogonal. If they were secretly the same construct, capacity could not have picked one and dropped the other.

Framed positively, higher capacity is associated with access to a wider repertoire of days. Capacity may not drive how any single day is executed, but it may set how many different kinds of days a person can reach. When that library shrinks toward one day repeated, behavioral flexibility is lost even if the person still navigates the home competently.

Next we asked whether day-similarity separates diagnostic groups directly.

Group means traced a tidy gradient from HOA (0.82) up through SCD (0.84) and MCI (0.88) to dementia (0.91). It is exactly the shape you would hope for. And it is not statistically significant (Kruskal-Wallis), because within-group spread is enormous: individual day-similarity ran from 0.60 to 0.98, and even among healthy adults some lived nearly identical days while others lived highly varied ones.

The salvage

The suggestive gradient tempts overreach. We report it as descriptive only and lean on the correlation instead, because that finding rests on continuous, individual-level data rather than four sparse group buckets (one of just 4 people). The honest reading is not "groups differ" but "diagnosis does not determine behavioral routine": the interesting variation lives within categories, which is precisely why an individual-level measure earns more trust than a group mean here.

Takeaway Working memory tracks how varied your days are, not how much of your home you use, and diagnostic label barely predicts either.

Coupled to time, stuck in space

The centerpiece is a second dissociation, this time within a single group. If routine and spatial reach were the same underlying decline, they should move together across the diagnostic gradient. They do not. In MCI they move in opposite directions.

Temporal coupling, how reliably a person is in the same place at the same hour every day, increases with impairment: HOA 0.90, SCD 0.90, MCI 0.94, dementia 0.95. The clock grip tightens. But spatial range, the number of distinct zones visited on a typical day, does not fall monotonically. It bottoms out in the middle: HOA visit 9.5 zones per day, dementia 9.8, and MCI the fewest at 7.7.

The move

Read those two panels together and a phenotype appears that neither shows alone. MCI participants hold a near-rigid clockwork routine (0.94, just below dementia's 0.95) and the smallest daily footprint: coupled to time, stuck in space. Temporal structure is preserved, even sharpened, while spatial exploration contracts. A one-dimensional "activity level" score would have averaged these into noise. Keeping time and space separate is what lets the contradiction become the finding.

This lines up with a strand of prior work suggesting temporal orientation can be relatively spared in early decline while spatial navigation erodes sooner. The contribution here is that the pattern reaches past internal orientation and into lived behavior: the MCI participants keep the schedule while the territory shrinks around them.

Clockwork routines within a contracted spatial world.the MCI phenotype

The individual trajectories put a human grain on the group averages and on the reframe that follows.

Participant 005 (dementia) held day-similarity high and flat, 0.94 to 0.97, across 15 months. The repertoire was already collapsed at the first observation and simply stayed collapsed. Participant 011 (SCD) sat lower and more variable, 0.62 to 0.71 over 14 months, with a slight upward drift.

The decision

Standard home-monitoring systems flag deviations from routine as the alarm. This flips the sign. If capacity buys variety, then the slow rise in participant 011's day-similarity, not any single anomalous event, is the thing to watch: it may be early routinization on the road to the collapsed pattern of 005. The warning sign is not the abnormal day. It is the gradual disappearance of different days.

We hold this loosely. High day-similarity could equally be adaptive early on: fewer decisions, more automaticity, a way to offload demand. Distinguishing helpful routine from pathological rigidity is unsettled, and one participant's stable trajectory cannot resolve it.

Takeaway Time and space dissociate in MCI, and routine collapse may be a signal to catch rather than a comfort to protect.

Where it stops

Where it stops. This is a small, exploratory, observational study, and the strongest claims in it are deliberately the narrow ones.

The cohort is 35 people, with only 4 in the dementia group. The day-similarity group gradient is non-significant, and that must not be read as evidence of no difference: the study simply lacked power to detect one. What earns confidence is the individual-level correlation between day-similarity and working memory, because it uses continuous per-person data rather than four sparse buckets. Even that explains only about 18% of the variance, so most of what drives daily routine lies elsewhere.

  • Capacity was measured by a single task, 1-back accuracy. Episodic memory, executive function, and processing speed could each relate to behavior differently, and this design cannot separate them.
  • Participants were community-dwelling and relatively high-functioning, so the picture may not extend to residential care or more severe impairment.
  • Behavior reflects more than cognition. Mobility limits, chronic conditions, personality, and home layout all move spatial range and day-similarity on their own; one participant's hoarding physically constrained navigable space. None of these were controlled.
  • It is observational. We cannot say whether routinization feeds decline, follows from it, or whether both trace a common cause.
The boundary

The deliberate trade was resolution over breadth: months of dense within-person trajectory instead of a one-time snapshot of a larger crowd. That is defensible for characterizing a new behavioral dimension, and it is the right posture for exploratory work. It is not a substitute for a powered, confounder-controlled replication, and we do not pretend otherwise.

What it opens

If routine and spatial reach are genuinely separate axes with separate ties to cognition, then both measurement and intervention can become more specific than "maintain the routine."

The practical inversion is the durable idea: monitoring could watch for rising day-similarity as an early marker of shrinking behavioral flexibility, rather than only alarming on departures from routine. Interventions could then target the specific deficit. For MCI, where the person is stuck in space but coupled to time, the intact clock becomes scaffolding: familiar-time cues could prompt movement into unused zones, using preserved temporal structure to carry recovered spatial reach. For dementia, where the repertoire is already collapsed, the aim shifts to introducing structured variation across days.

The move

The reframe is from surveillance to prosthesis. A smart home that only detects deviation treats the resident as a signal to be classified. A smart home that scaffolds novelty treats the failing person-environment coupling as the thing to support. Same sensors, opposite stance.

More broadly, the exercise here is about what a behavioral signal can and cannot be trusted to mean. Separating two conflated dimensions, showing one carries the cognitive relationship and one does not, and being explicit about which result is robust and which is merely suggestive, is the same discipline any behavioral marker needs before it can be trusted to guide a decision about a real person. The dissociations are a first map of the axes; the next step is to earn identifiability on each one, with a powered sample and the confounds measured rather than acknowledged.

Takeaway Two clean axes, one honest correlation, and a reframe: watch routine as a warning, and scaffold the specific dimension that is failing.
