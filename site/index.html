A research program in computational biology

When can we trust what a model says about a cell?

This is the question underneath the virtual cell. A prediction about how a cell will respond to a perturbation is only as good as our ability to tell genuine biology from confounding, and in high‑dimensional data, batch effects, selection, and gaps in the reference routinely manufacture structure that looks real. This work builds the identifiability layer that decides when a measurement can be believed, and states the boundary where it cannot.

The idea

One question, three fields

When is a measurement telling you about the world, and when is it telling you about your instrument?

That question is identifiability, and it is the connective tissue of this work. In high‑dimensional biology the danger is rarely too little signal. It is that the wrong thing looks exactly like signal: a batch effect, a selection bias, or a gap in a reference atlas can produce structure that passes every eye test and every standard metric, and a model that learns it will confidently propose the wrong answer.

Each study here follows the same discipline. First, separate the genuine biological signal from the confound that imitates it. Second, and this is the step most work skips, state the exact boundary where the two become impossible to separate, so a result is never trusted past the point the data can support. The aim is not a result. It is a result that carries, with it, the conditions under which it can be believed.

A model that confidently proposes the wrong target is worse than no model. from the research statement

In organoids

Thirty‑eight percent of an organoid atlas maps as off‑manifold. Rather than discard it, this work shows it is structured biology, a reference gap layered over a culture‑stress program, and marks the precise point where a clean lineage axis stops being recoverable at all.

separate the signal, then name the boundary

In proteomics

An apparent interaction between how different organs age shows up in all eighteen disease outcomes tested, then disappears once you account for which cohort each sample came from. The theory says why: the signal was assay‑batch structure leaking across views.

prove the leak, refuse the artifact

In behavior

Two features of daily life that look like one measurement. This work pulls them apart, daily routine versus how far a person ranges, and finds that in mild cognitive impairment the clock stays tight while the lived‑in world quietly contracts.

dissociate, then reinterpret

Research

Three studies, one discipline

Different data (single‑cell genomics, plasma proteomics, in‑home behavioral sensing) and one method throughout: certify what can be trusted, and name precisely what cannot. Each study is written to be read in full.

R1 Single‑cell genomics

NullState: quantifying the off‑target compartment of human neural organoids

Sole‑author study.

Brain organoids reliably grow cells that correspond to no real developmental state. This work turns that off‑target compartment from a known nuisance into a measured, decomposed, atlas‑anchored object, and states where the measurement itself runs out.

The problem

Human neural organoids have become a central model of brain development, yet they also produce cells that match no genuine in‑vivo neural state: non‑neural lineages from incomplete germ‑layer restriction, and aberrant states driven by the culture environment itself. The field lacked a principled way to answer three questions at once. Which organoid cells are off‑target? Why are they off‑target? And is that compartment merely noise, or does it carry reproducible biological structure? Reference‑based mapping offers the natural substrate: project organoid cells onto a comprehensive fetal atlas, and cells that map confidently to a real identity can be separated from those that do not.

The approach

NullState harmonizes two first‑trimester human references into a single 2,615,898‑cell atlas spanning neural and non‑neural origins, then maps the 1,767,346‑cell Human Neural Organoid Atlas onto it by variational reference surgery: an scVI model trained for 400 epochs, refined with scANVI, and projected with scArches. Every organoid cell receives two calibrated scores, an off‑manifold reconstruction distance and a label‑assignment entropy, and two thresholds fit on held‑out reference cells sort each cell into a six‑state taxonomy. Donor‑level integration lifts cross‑batch mixing (iLISI 6.10 to 8.10) while preserving biological structure (cLISI 1.31 to 1.44).

What it found

The dominant compartment is not an artifact. Across the atlas, 38.4% of cells fall into a large, largely neural off‑manifold state, and four independent diagnostics converge on real biology: these cells lose neural‑progenitor identity while gaining hypoxia and unfolded‑protein‑response stress signatures; their burden rises steadily with organoid age and metabolic load; it spans 0.4% to 96.8% across differentiation protocols in a way that tracks how far each protocol sits from an early‑cortex reference; and the cells concentrate into coherent transcriptomic territory rather than scattering. A direct test against an independent prenatal cortex atlas isolates a genuine in‑vitro‑divergence component over and above the reference gap.

  • 38.4% of cells are off‑manifold, decomposed into a reference‑coverage gap plus a pervasive in‑vitro stress program (hypoxia, unfolded‑protein response, glycolysis) rather than a mapping error.
  • A characterized negative result. A shared‑latent model provably cannot separate lineage from culture state here: an origin‑decoding adversary recovers organoid‑versus‑primary identity from the supposedly stripped latent at 0.998 balanced accuracy, and stays there when latent capacity is tripled or a batch covariate is added.
  • Where per‑cell disentanglement is impossible, relative geometry survives. Sliced‑Wasserstein distances cancel a shared source offset, and their rank order recovers the primary lineage structure (distance‑matrix correlation 0.90) across four well‑powered non‑neural lineages that clear a matched noise floor by 14 to 105 times.
What it establishes

The off‑target problem becomes a quantitative, atlas‑anchored measurement with two calibrated readouts and a clean decomposition. Just as important, the work names an identifiability boundary: in a regime where two domains occupy near‑disjoint manifolds, shared‑latent calibration without anchoring is not merely hard, it is undefined, and only encoder‑side or distributional methods can cross it. That boundary is offered as a contribution, not buried as a failed experiment.

Where it stops. Off‑manifold status conflates a reference‑coverage gap with genuine aberrance, so absolute off‑target rates are reference‑dependent and are read comparatively, never as fidelity scores. The recovered lineage geometry is validated at the level of rank order, not as a clean per‑pair distance: a direct test shows the shared offset is not constant across lineages (mutual cosine 0.15), so individual distances stay confounded until per‑lineage offset removal or a matched‑primary embedding is added. The well‑powered geometry is on non‑neural populations; neural off‑target geometry awaits a reference that closes the coverage gap.

R2 Methods and proteomics

A completeness hierarchy for multi‑view interaction detection

with Junhao Wen, Columbia University and the New York Genome Center.

When several partial measurements describe the same person, do the interactions between them carry information the individual measurements miss? This work gives the theory that answers when, and a large‑scale demonstration of how the question goes wrong.

The problem

Organ‑specific biological age gaps, derived from plasma proteomics, estimate how old each organ looks relative to a person’s actual age. As multi‑organ panels arrive, a natural question follows: beyond how each organ ages, does the joint pattern across organs, the interaction, predict disease? This is an instance of a general multi‑view problem, and the field leans on heuristics, most commonly view disagreement, without a rigorous account of what each heuristic can and cannot detect. The danger is that the datasets where the question is now asked, large consortia assembled from many cohorts on different assay platforms, have exactly the structure most likely to manufacture a false answer.

The approach

The work defines an operational hierarchy of multi‑view combinations, from individual‑view predictions and their disagreement, through explicit cross‑view products, to a joint nonparametric estimator, and proves three results about it. It then validates the hierarchy in simulation and applies it to the Global Neurodegeneration Proteomics Consortium: nine validated organ age gaps against eighteen disease outcomes.

What it found
  • View disagreement is provably blind to interactions. It is a function of main effects only, and its covariance with any interaction component is exactly zero. This is not a matter of statistical power; the relevant covariance is identically zero.
  • A compression‑and‑order tradeoff bounds the highest interaction order recoverable from a given sample size and number of views, so richer interactions demand more data in a precise, quantified way.
  • When views share a hidden factor such as an assay batch, interaction signal leaks into the individual‑view predictions at a characterized rate, manufacturing apparent interactions where none exist.
  • On the consortium data, a naive pooled analysis appears to show that pairwise organ‑aging interactions improve prediction for all eighteen outcomes. Cross‑cohort replication reveals Simpson’s paradox: the effect vanishes under cohort adjustment (mean incremental AUC +0.020 → −0.0006), and a single‑cohort analysis is null to negative. The apparent effect was entirely between‑cohort batch structure, precisely the leakage regime the third theorem describes.
What it establishes

Two practical conclusions follow. Interaction detection in harmonized multi‑cohort biomarker data is confounded by assay‑batch structure unless that structure is explicitly controlled. And disagreement‑based analyses, however intuitive, cannot detect interactions at all. The hierarchy gives a map of which summaries answer which questions, and the demonstration shows what it costs to ignore it.

R3 Behavioral sensing and cognitive aging

Coupled to Time, Stuck in Space: dissociable behavioral dimensions in MCI and dementia

with Diane J. Cook and Maureen Schmitter‑Edgecombe, Washington State University.

Cognitive decline is usually scored by what a person can do in a test. This work asks a different question from continuous in‑home sensing, how a person actually inhabits their world, and finds two dimensions of it where the field assumed one.

The problem

Standard assessment treats cognition as an internal capacity, measured when prompted. An older, relational view treats it instead as the fit between a person and their environment. Continuous smart‑home sensing can see that fit directly: not what a person can do in a clinic, but what they actually do at home, day after day. The specific question here is how similarly a person lives their days, and whether that is separable from how widely they move through their space.

The approach

Thirty‑five community‑dwelling older adults were monitored by passive ambient sensors for four days to twenty‑two months, producing more than two million sensor events. An Unscented Kalman Filter turns raw sensor firings into continuous position, discretized into one‑meter zones. From these trajectories the work computes day‑similarity (how closely one day resembles another), spatial complexity, temporal coupling (being in the same place at the same time across days), and spatial range (distinct zones visited per day), and relates them to working‑memory capacity measured by an in‑home n‑back task.

What it found
  • Two dimensions, not one. Working‑memory capacity is significantly, negatively related to day‑similarity: higher capacity goes with more varied days. It has no significant relationship with spatial complexity. The two behavioral dimensions are largely independent, so routine and spatial richness are separate axes.
  • A time‑and‑space dissociation in mild cognitive impairment: temporal coupling is highest short of dementia (0.94) while daily spatial range is the lowest of any group (7.7 zones). The clock stays tight even as the lived‑in territory contracts.
  • Individual trajectories across more than a year show the pattern forming: one participant already collapsed into near‑identical days, another varying but slowly routinizing, a plausible early signature of the same process.
What it establishes

Routinization and spatial complexity are distinct dimensions of behavioral organization with different relationships to cognitive capacity, which reframes what in‑home monitoring should watch for. If the collapse into routine is itself an early sign, the goal of an intervention is not to preserve routine but to scaffold variety, and for the dissociation seen here, to support spatial exploration while the intact temporal structure carries it.

Where it stops. The cohort is modest, especially for dementia, so group comparisons are underpowered and the descriptive gradient in day‑similarity is not statistically significant; the robust result is the individual‑level correlation, which still leaves most of the variance unexplained. A single cognitive measure is used, the study is observational, and behavior reflects more than cognition: mobility, home size, and health all shape spatial range independently. These are named as confounds, not controlled away.

Where it leads

Certifiable cell‑state control

The long arc of this work is a computational design engine for programmable in‑vivo genetic medicine: a system that selects a cell‑type‑specific target, predicts how a perturbation moves a cell from a diseased toward a healthy state, and helps design the molecule that carries it out. The binding constraint on that engine is not predictive power. It is trust. In recent work, independent groups using different metrics reached opposite conclusions about whether current models even beat simple baselines, because they were quietly measuring different, under‑specified quantities. The next stage builds the identifiability‑based certification layer underneath the engine, and advances up a deliberate ladder where each rung earns the next.

Methods

The recurring machinery

A compact, transferable toolkit runs through every study: the same moves, pointed at organoids, proteomes, or behavior.

Variational reference mapping

scVI, scANVI, and scArches surgery to co‑embed millions of cells against harmonized reference atlases.

Optimal transport and cancellation

Sliced‑Wasserstein distances whose invariance to a shared translation cancels a common confound, recovering relative geometry that per‑cell methods lose.

Adversarial confound decoding

Train an adversary to recover the nuisance you tried to remove. If it succeeds, the latent was never clean. A negative result with teeth.

Matched nulls and noise floors

Compare every distance against a calibrated null built from the data’s own structure. Nothing counts until it clears the floor.

Identifiability and causal representation

State the estimand and its identifying assumptions, drawing the line between what is recoverable and what is manufactured.

Rank‑level and distributional readouts

When a per‑cell coordinate is undefined, fall back to relative geometry and rank order, which degrade gracefully where point estimates fail.
